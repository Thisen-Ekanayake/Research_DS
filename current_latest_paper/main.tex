\begin{abstract}
Underemployment in Sri Lanka, encompassing time-related and
qualification-based forms, remains a heavily underanalysed dimension of
labour market slack. While headline unemployment stays near 3.8\%, quarterly
LFS micro-data reveals crisis peaks of 32.6\% (2020~Q2) and 31.1\%
(2021~Q2). We investigate macroeconomic drivers over 2015--2025 via
structural break detection (Zivot-Andrews, Bai-Perron PELT), Granger
causality, ARDL bounds testing, and XGBoost with SHAP interpretability.
The dataset incorporates remittances, agricultural output index,
part-time employment, discouraged workers, and a real exchange rate series
(annual CBSL averages, 2015--2024). GDP contraction and informal employment
emerge as dominant SHAP drivers. ARDL bounds testing on quarterly data
($n{=}36$) confirms long-run cointegration at 1\% ($F{=}7.25$, Pesaran
critical value 4.68); the ECM speed-of-adjustment $\lambda{=}{-1.087}$
($p{=}0.003$) indicates near-complete quarterly correction toward
equilibrium. A sensitivity analysis confirms strong convergence of macro
driver rankings across hours-based and qualification-based underemployment
definitions ($\rho = 0.893$, $p = 0.007$). Gender-disaggregated SHAP
reveals distinct driver structures by sex, with a statistically persistent
female excess (Wilcoxon $p = 0.001$) and widening qualification gender gap.
District-level analysis confirms spatial heterogeneity beyond informality.
The 2022 sovereign default structurally altered GDP and inflation
elasticities, confirming underemployment as a far more sensitive labour
market barometer than headline unemployment.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{Underemployment, Sri Lanka, ARDL, XGBoost, SHAP, Structural Breaks, Sensitivity Analysis, Gender Disaggregation}

\section{Introduction}
%% ===================================================

%% FIX 1: Em-dashes replaced with commas in prose
Sri Lanka's 2022 sovereign default, marked by foreign exchange collapse
and inflation exceeding 70\%~\cite{cbsl2025}, exposed a core flaw in
relying on aggregate unemployment as the primary labour indicator. With
unemployment stable at 3.8--5.5\% throughout, systemic underutilisation
was concealed. Two forms of underemployment surged: \textbf{time-related
underemployment} (workers below 40~hrs/week seeking more hours), rising
from 4.6\% (2019) to 8.3\% annually, but reaching 32.6\% in 2020~Q2 and
31.1\% in 2021~Q2 in quarterly series~\cite{dcs2025}; and
\textbf{qualification-based underemployment} (workers below their
educational level), driven by graduate mismatches and public sector
freezes.

Despite being the primary driver of post-recession wage
suppression~\cite{bell2018underemployment}, underemployment is absent from
Sri Lanka's labour literature, which stops at 2020~Q4~\cite{pabasara2025impact}.
This is the first study applying SHAP-based interpretability to Sri Lankan
underemployment dynamics across the full crisis arc, and the first to
include remittances, agricultural output, part-time employment, discouraged
workers, and a full real exchange rate series in the predictor set. We
address four research questions: \textbf{RQ1}~how underemployment evolved
2015--2025; \textbf{RQ2}~which indicators associate most strongly;
\textbf{RQ3}~whether 2022 structurally altered these relationships;
\textbf{RQ4}~evidence for long-run equilibrium.

To the best of our knowledge, this study represents the first effort
to: (1) apply SHAP-based machine learning interpretability to Sri Lankan
underemployment dynamics across the complete 2015--2025 crisis arc;
(2) simultaneously incorporate remittances, agricultural output, part-time
employment, and discouraged workers as predictors; (3) conduct
gender-disaggregated SHAP analysis revealing persistent female
underemployment gaps; and (4) test structural breaks across 11
macroeconomic and labour market series using complementary Zivot-Andrews
and Bai-Perron PELT methodologies. These advances directly address the
three limitations of Pabasara and Silva~\cite{pabasara2025impact}: their
exclusive focus on aggregate unemployment rather than underemployment,
termination of analysis at 2020~Q4 before the sovereign default, and
reliance on linear VECM specifications ill-suited to crisis-induced
nonlinearities.

\section{Related Work}
%% ===================================================

\subsection{Underemployment Theory and Measurement}
 
Underemployment matters distinctly from unemployment because it captures
hours-based slack and skills mismatch that persist post-recovery,
suppressing wages even as headline rates normalise~\cite{bell2018underemployment,ilo2013}.
Bell and Blanchflower~\cite{bell2018underemployment} develop an
Underemployment Index demonstrating this dynamic in the US and Europe,
where hours deficits linger long after unemployment recovers---directly
mirroring Sri Lanka's 3.8\% unemployment in 2023 amid persistently
elevated underemployment. The ILO 19th ICLS Resolution~\cite{ilo2013}
formalises the distinction between time-related underemployment (insufficient
hours) and qualification-based mismatch (skills-occupation gap), establishing
both as necessary components of a complete labour underutilisation account,
particularly in high-informality economies. Haider~\cite{haider2001} links
underemployment spells to long-term earnings instability even after
re-employment, while Autor~\cite{autor2014} connects qualification mismatch
to structural labour market polarisation---a mechanism directly relevant to
Sri Lanka's growing graduate overhang in a narrowing formal sector.
 
\subsection{Underemployment in Developing and Crisis Economies}
 
In crisis-hit economies, underemployment rises persistently due to structural
shifts in services-sector composition and youth labour force
entry~\cite{oecd2017}. The OECD~\cite{oecd2017} documents a 27\% average
increase across 29 countries post-2006, with the sharpest and most durable
spikes in economies undergoing fiscal consolidation; Sri Lanka aligns with
this pattern through its high youth and informal sector exposure. Flek and
Mysikova~\cite{flek2015} show that low-skilled workers in Central European
post-crisis economies cycle repeatedly through underemployment rather than
recovering to full employment---a hysteresis dynamic consistent with Sri
Lanka's sustained post-2022 underemployment elevation. Regionally,
Chowdhury~\cite{chowdhury2009} provides the most relevant survey,
documenting underemployment determinants across Bangladesh, India, Indonesia,
and Thailand and attributing persistence to weak aggregate demand and
informality, providing the regional baseline absent from Sri Lankan
studies. Islam and Verick~\cite{islam2011} analyse labour market recovery
following sovereign debt crises, identifying delayed transmission of fiscal
contractions into hours-based slack---consistent with our 2--3 quarter TLCC
lags. Unlike Greece's 2010 sovereign debt crisis---where underemployment
exhibited five-year hysteresis with only partial mean
reversion~\cite{oecd2017}---Sri Lanka's post-2022 trajectory shows recovery
capacity while remaining structurally elevated above pre-crisis baselines.
 
\subsection{Sri Lankan Labour Market Context}
 
For Sri Lanka, Pabasara and Silva~\cite{pabasara2025impact} provide the
closest precedent via VECM (2005--2020~Q4), confirming Okun's Law on
aggregate unemployment but using headline unemployment as the dependent
variable, terminating before the 2022 crisis, and applying only linear
methods---three limitations the present study directly addresses.
Dissanayake~\cite{dissanayake2026} provides a SARIMA and MLR baseline
(1991--2022) that explicitly calls for crisis-updated forecasts and
extension to underemployment hours, directly motivating this work.
Perera~\cite{perera2022} establishes the agricultural sector as the
primary source of seasonal inactive hours via LFS 2018 micro-data,
supporting inclusion of the Agricultural Output Index. The Central Bank
of Sri Lanka Annual Report~\cite{cbsl2025} documents the 2022 crisis's
labour market disruptions, providing official context against which our
structural break findings can be benchmarked; our empirical identification
of breaks at 2021--2022 confirms and quantifies the CBSL's qualitative
narrative. The ILO World Employment and Social Outlook~\cite{iloweso2024}
further contextualises these findings within global post-COVID labour
market dynamics.
 
To the best of our knowledge, no prior Sri Lanka study applies machine
learning or SHAP-based interpretability to underemployment forecasting,
spans the complete 2015--2025 crisis arc, simultaneously models remittances,
agricultural output, part-time employment, and discouraged workers, conducts
gender-disaggregated SHAP analysis, or tests structural breaks across
11 series using both Zivot-Andrews and Bai-Perron PELT methodologies.

\section{Data and Methodology}

\subsection{Dataset and Variables}

We construct an annual panel ($n{=}10$, 2015--2024) for econometric
estimation and a quarterly dataset ($n{\approx}40$, 2015~Q1--2024~Q4)
for machine learning diagnostics and ARDL bounds testing.
Table~\ref{tab:variables} summarises all ten variables. Each variable
was selected on theoretical grounds rather than availability alone; we
briefly motivate each choice below.

\textbf{Target variable.} \emph{Time-related underemployment} (DCS
Quarterly Labour Force Survey) captures workers available for and seeking
additional hours beyond 40~hrs/week. Bell and
Blanchflower~\cite{bell2018underemployment} demonstrate that this
measure more faithfully reflects post-recession wage suppression than
headline unemployment, motivating its use as the dependent variable
throughout.

\textbf{Demand-side predictors.} \emph{Real GDP Growth Rate}
(CBSL/World Bank) serves as the aggregate demand proxy; a contraction
reduces available working hours before triggering outright layoffs.
\emph{Inflation Rate} (CBSL CCPI, year-on-year) captures the
price-level channel through which real wage erosion forces
involuntary part-time acceptance.

\textbf{Labour market structure.} \emph{Informal Employment} (\% of
total employment, DCS LFS) captures the structural buffer where
surplus labour is absorbed without formal contracts, a channel
identified by ILO~\cite{ilo2013,iloweso2024} as the primary locus of
hours-based underutilisation in economies where informality exceeds
60\%. \emph{Youth LFPR} (ages 15--24, DCS LFS/ILO) proxies
qualification mismatch: rising youth participation without
corresponding formal job creation drives over-qualification and
involuntary part-time work~\cite{oecd2017}. \emph{Part-time Employment}
(\% of total employment, ILO) directly measures the
short-hours margin. \emph{Discouraged Workers} (DCS LFS Table~3.13)
captures hidden labour slack not reflected in either unemployment or
underemployment statistics.

\textbf{External and sectoral channels.} \emph{Remittances} (World
Bank, current USD) represent the income-smoothing mechanism
whereby diaspora transfers cushion household consumption and reduce
pressure to accept inadequate hours during downturns. \emph{Agricultural
Output Index} (FAO, weighted composite) captures the seasonal
employment channel: Perera~\cite{perera2022} establishes agriculture
as the primary source of seasonal inactive hours via LFS 2018
micro-data. \emph{Real Exchange Rate} (LKR/USD, constructed from CBSL
and FRED daily data, CPI-deflated) captures the competitiveness and
import-cost channel through which currency depreciation affects
tradeable-sector employment hours.

%% 3.1.1 Real Exchange Rate Construction
%% ---------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Real Exchange Rate Construction}

A consistent real LKR/USD series does not exist from any single
source for 2015--2024. We construct one by splicing two nominal
sub-series and deflating with bilateral CPI ratios:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{RER}_t = e_t^{\text{nom}} \times
  \frac{\text{CPI}_t^{\text{USA}}}{\text{CPI}_t^{\text{LKA}}}
  \label{eq:rer}
\end{equation}
%
where $e_t^{\text{nom}}$ is the nominal LKR/USD mid-rate.

\textbf{Nominal series (2015 Q1--2020 Q4).} Daily mid-rates are
obtained from the CBSL web data portal and aggregated to quarterly
means using business-day averaging.

\textbf{Nominal series (2021 Q1--2024 Q4).} Daily rates are sourced
from FRED series DEXSLUS~\cite{cbsl2025} and aggregated identically.

\textbf{Splice diagnostic.} The two sub-series do not formally
overlap. The CBSL 2020~Q4 mean is 185.30~LKR/USD; the FRED 2021~Q1
mean is 194.23~LKR/USD, a gap of 8.93~LKR. This exceeds our
pre-registered $\pm 2$~LKR threshold, consistent with the CBSL
methodology change in late 2020 when the Central Bank shifted from a
managed peg toward a more flexible exchange rate regime. Because this
level shift is a genuine structural policy event rather than a data
artefact, no manual intercept adjustment is applied; instead the
exchange rate series is retained with the splice point documented
for transparency.

\textbf{Deflation.} The Sri Lanka CPI (CBSL CCPI, 2013 base) and
US CPI (FRED series CPIAUCSL) are each resampled to quarterly
frequency and applied via Equation~\ref{eq:rer}. The resulting real
rate captures both nominal depreciation and differential inflation,
isolating the competitiveness channel relevant to tradeable-sector
employment hours.

%% 3.1.2 The 2022 LFS Artefact
%% ---------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{The 2022 LFS Reporting Artefact}

The 2022 annual LFS reports time-related underemployment at 2.3\%, an
implausibly low figure during a year of sovereign default, foreign
exchange rationing, and inflation exceeding 70\%. We attribute this to
social desirability bias compounded by genuine confusion among
respondents about whether additional hours were available during the
acute cash shortage~\cite{dcs2025}.

Our mitigation strategy comprises three components:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \textbf{Primary series (quarterly):} We use the quarterly
    LFS data for 2022~Q1--Q4, where shorter enumeration windows
    reduce the annual reporting bias. The quarterly and annual
    figures diverge anomalously only in 2022 (quarterly mean:
    $\approx$3.2\% vs.\ annual report: 2.3\%).
  \item \textbf{Imputed annual value:} For models requiring the
    annual series, the 2022 figure is replaced with the simple
    average of 2021 and 2023 annual values:
    $\hat{U}_{2022} = (U_{2021} + U_{2023}) / 2$, explicitly
    flagged as \texttt{LFS artefact --- imputed}.
  \item \textbf{Sensitivity check:} All econometric models are
    estimated twice: once with raw 2022 data (2.3\%) and once with
    the imputed value. Results are robust; marginal differences
    are reported in the supplementary material.
\end{enumerate}

%% 3.1.3 Imputation Methodology
%% ---------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Missing Data and Imputation}

Of the $10 \times 10 = 100$ variable-year cells in the annual panel,
two observations are missing, concentrated in 2024 (GDP Growth
Rate and Real GDP). We apply a dual-method imputation framework:

\textbf{KNN imputation} (scikit-learn \texttt{KNNImputer},
$k{=}3$, distance-weighted) is applied to isolated single-point gaps
where adjacent observations provide reliable local information.

\textbf{MICE imputation} (scikit-learn \texttt{IterativeImputer},
$m{=}5$ multiply-imputed datasets, 20 iterations each, median
initialisation) is applied to block gaps, typically consecutive
missing values at series endpoints. Following Rubin's
rules~\cite{vanbuuren2018}, point estimates are pooled as the
arithmetic mean across all five imputed datasets. The method
selection (KNN vs.\ MICE) is determined programmatically: if a
missing value has no adjacent missing neighbours it is classified
as isolated (KNN); otherwise it is treated as part of a block (MICE).

Every imputed value is logged in a machine-readable audit report
documenting: the variable and time period, the method applied and
its rationale, the imputed value alongside the plausible range
from adjacent observations, and whether the value was used in the
annual model, quarterly model, or both. Imputed values are flagged
in the supplementary dataset.

%% 3.1.4 Temporal Disaggregation
%% ---------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Temporal Disaggregation}

Several predictors are available only at annual frequency
(remittances, agricultural output). Rather than applying naive
linear interpolation, we use the Denton-Cholette
method~\cite{denton1971}, which distributes annual totals across
quarters while preserving the movement pattern of a related
quarterly indicator series. Specifically:

\textbf{Remittances.} Monthly CBSL worker remittance data serves as
the quarterly indicator; the Denton-Cholette procedure ensures that
$\sum_{q=1}^{4} \hat{R}_{t,q} = R_t^{\text{annual}}$ for each year
$t$.

\textbf{Agricultural output.} The quarterly indicator is constructed
from DCS paddy production data disaggregated by cropping season
(Maha: October--March harvest, mapped to Q1; Yala: April--September
harvest, mapped to Q3), with Q2 and Q4 assigned a baseline
inter-seasonal value. The Denton-Cholette procedure then adjusts
these seasonal indicators to match the annual FAO index total while
preserving the within-year seasonal pattern.

Both disaggregated series pass the summation identity check:
quarterly aggregates match annual totals to within machine precision
($< 10^{-4}$).

%% 3.1.5 Agricultural Output Index
%% ---------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Agricultural Output Index Construction}

The initial dataset contained 60+ granular FAO crop columns
(individual indices for cabbages, carrots, avocados, etc.). We
replace these with a single weighted composite index covering
the five crop groups that dominate Sri Lankan agricultural
employment: paddy (rice), tea, rubber, coconut, and vegetables.

Weights are derived empirically from FAO Gross Value of Production
at constant 2014--2016 international USD prices, averaged over
2015--2024. Let $V_c$ denote the mean annual value of production for
crop group $c$; the weight assigned to each group is:
%
\begin{equation}
  w_c = \frac{V_c}{\sum_{c'} V_{c'}}
  \label{eq:agri_weight}
\end{equation}
%
This value-of-production weighting is more defensible than an
equal-weight or area-based composite because it reflects the
economic significance of each crop to agricultural GDP and aligns
with the DCS LFS agricultural employment categories. Note that
``Vegetables'' aggregates multiple FAO sub-items (e.g., beans,
onions, chillies) into a single group before weight computation.

The resulting weighted index, \texttt{AgriProdIdx\_Weighted}, is
used as the sole agricultural predictor in all subsequent models,
replacing the 60+ individual crop indices.

\subsubsection{Reproducible Pipeline}

The full data pipeline is implemented as a sequence of Python
scripts, executable from scratch by any team member:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, nosep]
  \item \texttt{03\_exchange\_rate.py}: CBSL/FRED splice and
    CPI-deflated real rate (Eq.~\ref{eq:rer}).
  \item \texttt{04\_temp\_disagg.py}: Denton-Cholette temporal
    disaggregation of annual-only series.
  \item \texttt{05\_artefact\_handling.py}: 2022 LFS artefact
    mitigation and sensitivity dataset generation.
  \item \texttt{04\_imputation.py}: KNN/MICE imputation with
    Rubin's rule pooling and audit log generation.
  \item \texttt{07\_agri\_index.py}: FAO value-of-production
    weighted agricultural index construction
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:agri_weight}).
\end{enumerate}

All scripts, raw data, and the imputation audit log are provided
as supplementary material. The pipeline produces two clean output
datasets: an annual panel (\texttt{master\_dataset\_final.csv},
$n{=}10$) and a quarterly panel
(\texttt{quarterly\_agricultural\_output.csv}), both used in all
subsequent analyses. A machine-readable data dictionary
(\texttt{data\_dictionary.md}) documents every variable's source,
unit, frequency, access date, and any transformations applied.

\subsection{Econometric Pipeline}

\textbf{Stationarity.} ADF and KPSS tests are applied in levels and first
differences~\cite{kpss1992}. Most predictors are I(1); all ambiguous series
become stationary in first differences. The exchange rate shows evidence of
I(2) consistent with its structural devaluation trajectory; it is retained
in the SHAP model but treated with caution in the ARDL specification.

\textbf{Granger Causality and ARDL.} On the annual dataset ($n{=}10$,
maxlag=1, Bonferroni $\alpha{=}0.01$) no predictor clears the corrected
threshold; Youth LFPR is suggestive at nominal $\alpha{=}0.10$
($p{=}0.082$). On the quarterly dataset ($n{=}36$, maxlag=4, Bonferroni
$\alpha{=}0.05/7{=}0.0071$), Agricultural Output Index is the only
Granger-cause clearing the corrected threshold ($F{=}11.91$, $p{=}0.0016$);
GDP Growth is suggestive at nominal $\alpha{=}0.10$ ($p{=}0.080$).

The ARDL($p,q$) specification for the long-run relationship is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \Delta U_t = \alpha_0
    + \sum_{i=1}^{p}\phi_i\,\Delta U_{t-i}
    + \sum_{j=0}^{q}\boldsymbol{\beta}_j'\,\Delta\mathbf{x}_{t-j}
    + \lambda_1 U_{t-1}
    + \boldsymbol{\lambda}_2'\mathbf{x}_{t-1}
    + \varepsilon_t
  \label{eq:ardl}
\end{equation}
%
where $\lambda_1$ and $\boldsymbol{\lambda}_2$ are the long-run level
coefficients tested via Pesaran's $F$-bounds procedure~\cite{pesaran2001}.
AIC selects ARDL(3,1,1,2,0) on the quarterly dataset with seasonal
dummies included. The bounds $F$-statistic is $7.25$ ($p{=}0.0008$),
exceeding the 1\% upper critical value of $4.68$ for $k{=}5$ predictors
(Case~III: unrestricted intercept, no trend), confirming long-run
cointegration. If cointegration holds, the ECM representation is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \Delta U_t = \mu + \lambda\,\widehat{\text{ECT}}_{t-1}
    + \sum_{i}\psi_i\,\Delta U_{t-i}
    + \sum_{j}\boldsymbol{\gamma}_j'\,\Delta\mathbf{x}_{t-j}
    + \varepsilon_t
  \label{eq:ecm}
\end{equation}
%
where $\widehat{\text{ECT}}_{t-1} = U_{t-1} - \hat\alpha_{LR}
- \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}'\mathbf{x}_{t-1}$ is the estimated error
correction term and $\lambda$ is the speed-of-adjustment coefficient.

\subsection{Machine Learning and Interpretability}

%% FIX 1: Em-dash replaced with comma
XGBoost~\cite{chen2016xgboost} is fitted on an 80/20 temporal split with
the full nine-variable feature set (Table~\ref{tab:variables}). Exchange
rate is retained in the ML model, unlike the ARDL specification, since
tree models are not sensitive to multicollinearity. SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017unified}
decomposes each prediction into additive feature contributions:
%
\begin{equation}
  \hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{M} \phi_i(x_i)
  \label{eq:shap}
\end{equation}
%
where $\phi_0$ is the base (mean) prediction and $\phi_i$ is the Shapley
value of feature $i$, computed as the weighted average of marginal
contributions across all feature coalitions. SHAP values are
cross-validated against OLS coefficient directions to ensure consistency
across methods. A waterfall chart and force plot for the 2022 crisis peak
year are generated (Figure~\ref{fig:shap_waterfall}), providing the
per-observation SHAP decomposition. The sensitivity analysis
(Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}) cross-validates SHAP rankings across both
underemployment definitions.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Underemployment Dynamics}

Figure~\ref{fig:macro_trends} shows unemployment remaining flat at
3.8--5.5\% while underemployment surges during 2020--2022. Annual values
mask the true crisis intensity: quarterly extraction yields peaks of 32.6\%
(2020~Q2) and 31.1\% (2021~Q2), with sustained 23--28\% through 2022.
Female underemployment consistently exceeds male throughout the full period
(3.8\% vs.\ 2.0\% in 2015; 2.5\% vs.\ 2.0\% in 2024), with the gap widening
at crisis peak. The STL trend component confirms that the post-crisis level
(2.2\% annual, 2024) remains elevated relative to the pre-crisis baseline
of 2.7\% (2015 average), indicating incomplete structural recovery.

\subsection{Structural Break and Interaction Results}

%% FIX 1: Em-dashes replaced with commas
Bai-Perron PELT detects a single dominant break in underemployment at 2022,
with co-occurring breaks in inflation (2021) and exchange rate (2021)
preceding the labour market response by approximately one year. To quantify
this elasticity shift, we estimate the post-break interaction model:
%
\begin{equation}
  U_t = \alpha + \beta_1\,G_t + \beta_2\,D_t + \beta_3\,(G_t \times D_t) + \varepsilon_t
  \label{eq:interaction}
\end{equation}
%
where $G_t$ is GDP Growth, $D_t{=}\mathbf{1}(t{\geq}2022)$ is the crisis
dummy, and $\beta_3$ captures the post-break amplification of GDP shocks on
underemployment. The GDP interaction model achieves $R^2{=}0.495$ and the
inflation model $R^2{=}0.594$ (Figure~\ref{fig:interaction}). Informal
employment breaks significantly at 2017 ($t{=}{-6.02}$, $p{<}0.01$),
three years before the crisis, suggesting that formalisation pressures
pre-date the sovereign default. Part-time employment breaks at 2020
($t{=}{-5.43}$, $p{<}0.05$), consistent with a COVID-driven shift toward
shorter working arrangements.

\subsection{ARDL Bounds Test and Error Correction Model}
\label{sec:ardl}

The quarterly master dataset ($n{=}36$ after the four-quarter YoY lag,
2016~Q1--2024~Q4) is assembled by applying Denton-Cholette temporal
disaggregation to annual-only series (Real~GDP, CPI, Informal Employment,
Youth~LFPR) and merging with directly observed quarterly series
(underemployment, exchange rate, remittances, agricultural output index).
ADF unit root tests confirm Underemployment, Remittances, and Exchange Rate
are I(1); Informal Employment and Youth LFPR are I(0); GDP Growth Rate
shows I(2)$+$ behaviour (retained in ML model, first-differenced for ARDL).

\textbf{Bounds test.} AIC selects ARDL(3,1,1,2,0) with quarterly seasonal
dummies. The Pesaran $F$-bounds statistic is $\mathbf{F{=}7.25}$
($p{=}0.0008$), exceeding the 1\% I(1) upper bound of 4.68 for $k{=}5$
regressors (Case~III)~\cite{pesaran2001}. Cointegration is confirmed at 1\%.

\textbf{Long-run coefficients.} The long-run equilibrium relationship
implies: GDP Growth ($\hat\theta{=}{-}0.393$), Inflation
($\hat\theta{=}{-}0.142$), Youth LFPR ($\hat\theta{=}{+}0.260$), and
Remittances ($\hat\theta{\approx}0$). A 1~pp increase in GDP growth reduces
long-run underemployment by 0.39~pp; rising youth participation increases
it by 0.26~pp.

\textbf{ECM (Eq.~\ref{eq:ecm}).} The speed-of-adjustment coefficient
$\hat\lambda{=}{-}1.087$ ($p{=}0.003$, 95\% CI $[-1.81,\,-0.37]$) is
negative and significant at 1\%, confirming error correction. The magnitude
near unity indicates near-complete correction within one quarter. ECM
diagnostics confirm model adequacy: Durbin-Watson~$=1.94$; Breusch-Pagan
$p{=}0.11$ (homoskedastic); Ljung-Box $Q(4)$ $p{=}0.95$ (no serial
correlation); Jarque-Bera $p{=}0.62$ (normal residuals); $R^2{=}0.765$.
This answers \textbf{RQ4}: a long-run equilibrium between underemployment
and its macroeconomic drivers exists and deviations correct rapidly.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis: Underemployment Definitions}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

A parallel XGBoost-SHAP analysis was conducted on the qualification-based

both models. The main divergence is Inflation, which rises from rank~6
(hours-based) to rank~4 (qualification-based), consistent with

\section{Policy Recommendations}
%% ===================================================
 
Our analysis identifies four high-impact, evidence-based interventions
targeting the SHAP-derived drivers of underemployment. These are
concrete, agency-specific, and designed for Sri Lanka's post-crisis
fiscal context.
 
\textbf{Formalisation via social protection extension.}
Informal employment ranks as the second-largest SHAP driver, with a
structural break at 2017 indicating formalisation pressures pre-dating
the 2022 default. The Employees' Provident Fund (EPF) and Employees'
Trust Fund (ETF)---currently formal-sector only---should be extended
via a simplified contributory tier for informal workers (approximately
60\% of the workforce). A pilot with tripartite matching contributions
for micro-enterprises employing fewer than ten workers would begin
closing this structural gap. ILO evidence~\cite{iloweso2024} indicates
that countries extending social protection to informal workers during
crises experienced 15--20\% smaller underemployment spikes.
 
\textbf{Youth qualification matching.}
Youth LFPR (SHAP rank~3) shows suggestive Granger causality
($p{=}0.082$), consistent with qualification mismatch as young
graduates accept under-matched employment or exit the labour force.
Expansion of the existing Graduate Entrepreneurship Programme,
combined with a digital job-matching platform linking universities to
private sector roles requiring graduate skills, would directly target
this channel. The World Bank Sri Lanka Human Capital
Review~\cite{worldbank2023} identifies TVET reform as the highest-priority
structural intervention for graduate underemployment reduction.
 
\textbf{Remittance stabilisation mechanism.}
Remittances exhibit a negative lagged association with underemployment;
the 2021 collapse from \$7.0~bn to \$5.5~bn preceded the peak
underemployment quarter, consistent with diaspora transfers
functioning as a private income buffer. The Central Bank of Sri Lanka
should implement an automatic stabiliser: when quarterly remittance
inflows fall below a threshold (\$1.5~bn per quarter), a matching
subsidy programme activated from existing exchange equalisation
reserves would partially replace this cushion. This mirrors the design
principles of the IMF Resilience and Sustainability Facility applied
to private capital flows.
 
\textbf{Adoption of ILO labour underutilisation framework.}
The 2022 LFS artefact---underemployment reported at 2.3\% due to
social desirability bias during the cash shortage---demonstrates that
headline unemployment is an inadequate crisis barometer. The Department
of Census and Statistics should adopt the ILO's composite Labour
Underutilisation Framework (LU1--LU4 indicators) as quarterly headline
statistics alongside unemployment, with explicit disaggregation into
time-related and skills-related components. This aligns with the 19th
ICLS Resolution~\cite{ilo2013} and OECD best practice.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ===================================================

Quarterly LFS micro-data reveals crisis underemployment peaks of 32.6\%,
nine times the concurrent 3.8\% unemployment rate, confirming that
aggregate unemployment is an inadequate crisis barometer for Sri Lanka.
Structural break tests confirm 2021--2022 as a statistically significant
rupture in the underemployment--GDP--inflation system, with macro breaks
leading the labour market break by one year. The incorporated variables
reveal two additional findings: informal employment broke significantly
in 2017, predating the crisis and suggesting formalisation pressure as
a structural precondition; and part-time employment spiked in 2020
under COVID, acting as a precursor to the 2022 hours-underemployment
surge. SHAP (Eq.~\ref{eq:shap}) establishes GDP contraction and informal
employment as the dominant drivers, with Youth LFPR, remittances, and
part-time employment capturing complementary channels. The interaction
model (Eq.~\ref{eq:interaction}) confirms structural amplification of
GDP elasticity post-2022. The 2--3 quarter TLCC lags are consistent
with a labour hoarding transmission mechanism, and hysteresis analysis
indicates that underemployment remained structurally elevated through
2024 despite partial macroeconomic recovery.
 
ARDL bounds testing on the quarterly dataset ($n{=}36$) resolves
\textbf{RQ4}: the Pesaran $F$-statistic of 7.25 confirms long-run
cointegration at 1\%, and the ECM speed-of-adjustment
$\hat\lambda{=}{-}1.087$ ($p{=}0.003$) indicates near-complete quarterly
correction toward equilibrium. Long-run GDP elasticity is $-0.39$~pp
per 1~pp GDP growth. The sensitivity analysis confirms SHAP rankings
are strongly convergent across hours-based and qualification-based
definitions ($\rho = 0.893$, $p = 0.007$). Gender-disaggregated
analysis reveals distinct driver structures: female underemployment is
more inflation-sensitive while male underemployment responds primarily
to GDP contractions. The persistent female--male gap ($W = 55.0$,
$p = 0.001$) and widening qualification gender gap (0.97~pp to 4.42~pp)
indicate the crisis disproportionately affected women's labour market
outcomes. District-level analysis confirms substantial spatial
heterogeneity that informality alone does not explain. Four targeted
policy interventions---EPF/ETF extension, graduate job matching, a
remittance stabilisation mechanism, and DCS adoption of the ILO
LU1--LU4 framework---address both demand-side and supply-side channels
while upgrading statistical infrastructure.
 
\textbf{Limitations.}
This analysis confronts five primary constraints. First, the annual
XGBoost model ($n{=}10$) imposes a binding degrees-of-freedom constraint
that precludes Johansen cointegration testing and full multi-regressor
ARDL estimation on the annual dataset; the quarterly panel ($n{\approx}43$)
substantially mitigates this. Second, SHAP values capture predictive
association, not causal direction: high informal employment importance
may reflect common shocks driving both informality expansion and hours
contraction simultaneously rather than a direct causal path. Third,
real wages and sectoral employment shares (manufacturing, services,
agriculture) were excluded due to insufficient quarterly availability
for 2015--2025, despite being theoretically important transmission
channels. Fourth, the 2022 LFS measurement artefact compromises annual
inference for the hours-based series even with the quarterly workaround;
results for that year should be interpreted with caution. Fifth, Sri
Lanka represents a specific small open economy configuration (sovereign
default combined with high remittance dependence), which may limit
external validity to other developing countries absent a comparable
crisis-remittance constellation. The ECM speed-of-adjustment
$|\hat\lambda|{>}1$ indicates mild overshooting, consistent with
volatile crisis-period dynamics, and should be interpreted cautiously.
 
\textbf{Future work} will: (i) re-estimate ARDL/ECM on the expanded
quarterly dataset ($n{\approx}43$) once 2025~Q3--Q4 LFS data are released;
(ii) incorporate the 2025 annual LFS data; (iii) extend gender analysis
to quarterly disaggregation for higher-power break detection; and
(iv) develop district-level panel models to explain spatial heterogeneity
beyond informality.
